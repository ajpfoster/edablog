Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset

Posted by Ash Foster on May 13th, 2019

Neuroplasticity

Understanding that the brain is physiologically a lot more malleable than we believed in the past is the key to benefitting from neuroplasticity. The scientific argument for practise making permanent in neural pathways of the brain lends credence to the old adage of practise making perfect and do you actually ever forget how to ride a bike?

For my own benefit in regards to neuroplasticity I have found contextual practise to be beneficial in most things I found difficult at first. Learning music at a young age, playing different sports and studying computers and math academically were all goals of mine but I would never call myself an allrounder nor have I ever really had a deadline or sign off date on those goals. I possibly benefited from a mix of teaching styles which pushed me to challenge myself, my strategies of learning and the status quo. I still remember not being able to play a bar chord on guitar, not knowing what the heck Julie Andrews singing "do-re-mi" for and I'm still terrible at tennis.

Learn a new language, do a sudoko, meditate, go for a run, do a crossword, read a book upside down, draw a little BIG picture, sing a jingle, do a ninja hand jutsu thing and dab in crocs - retrieving a memory, and reviewing material in a variety of ways helps build thicker, stronger, more hard-wired connections in the brain.

Growth Mindset

Growth mindset could be thought of as a framework for the better use of the neuroplasticity of the brain. Approaching problems and challenges with the perspective or attitude that failure does not equate to inability - that we and our brains have the capacity to grow skills and knowledge. It is relevant because education curriculum in the past has been focussed on rote learning and studies have shown that ranking or measuring a student's success in a fixed manner, to the students detriment, often results in promoting a fixed mindset.

I have explored growth mindset before but in its nature the more your explore the more you now know you do not know. I think better understanding this will benefit my motivation in applying healthy habits of growth for my learning journey at EDA.

I really resonated with Carol Dwecks perspective of not yet. This will guide my web development learning plan in that it firms up the need for a Just in Time approach to learning. In this context there is no way I will possibly be an expert in all areas of the tech stack in a small time frame but if I can teach myself to learn quickly I will be in a good position to continually improve.
